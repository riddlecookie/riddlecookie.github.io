Ladies and gentlemen. Boys and girls. Weeeeelcome, to the very first out of many (at least I hope out of many…) way too philosophical texts about way too simple things! Today, we are going to try to answer the question, whether there is such a thing as a “good person”. Or rather, along the way we will try to find some kind of moral compass, that could direct us in our everyday lives.
I waste way too much time thinking about such things, so I thought I could waste a little more and write this down just to share my thoughts with you. So, without any further ado, let’s start with some pretty basic stuff.

# What does it even mean, to be a "good person"?

We have some intuition behind this phrase, that is pretty solidly rooted in our society. A simple "recipe" would be not to cheat, steal, disrespect or kill others (so in general, **not to harm other living beings**). Well... I argue to say, that it's not that simple.

First of all, you have to ask yourself a very important question - "do I like chicken curry"? If the answer is "yes" I have some bad news for you. From the perspective of the chicken you are probably the most horrible thing walking on the surface of Earth. And I don't want in this place to try to convert you to vegetarianism (although it's a cool idea to consider), but rather to put emphasis on something different. On the **subjectivity** of the whole problem. From your perspective, especially if you don't have a huge knowledge about nutrition and dietetics, it's not that bad idea to buy this poor chicken, eat it and just stay healthy. From the perspective of the chicken the very same scenario doesn't look as good anymore. So there is a kind of dichotomy here.

At this point you may think "OK, so if everything is subjective and **there is no universal truth about the world**, why should I follow all the socially accepted norms, if I don't agree with them?". My answer to that question, would be not to ignore the wisdom of our predecessors and the power of evolution. It's just my wild guess, but I think that all of our today's moral standards are the result of a very, very long process, in which they evolved from our ancient ancestors' desire, to simply survive. Maybe along the way it appeared, that kindness and speaking the truth introduces the least amount of chaos into our world, which in turn increases the chances of our survival? I don't know that for sure, but this idea resonates with me really strongly.

# A little pinch of empathy

I think it's reasonable to extend our definition a little bit by adding some more empathy to it. Let's change "don't harm other living beings" into **"don't harm any other living beings"**. Small change, but huge consequences. And we can already say, that this goal is unreachable (at least with our current technology). You cannot make a step, without killing many, many microbes. Even if you would like to become a fruitarian in order to save as many beings as possible, the ground under the trees from which you would take fruits to eat would have to be prepared for cultivation, which also involves killing some living organisms. Finally, you may want to do something with the best intentions, but not foresee the long term impact of your actions, which could also harm others (we will come back to this topic later).

So wrapping it all up, it's impossible (at least in the times we live in) not to harm any other living beings. In fact I think, that **the very act of existence is associated with some kind of egoism**. But let's not dive too deep into this topic. Instead, let's focus back again on our "recipe". If it's impossible to not harm other beings, let's redefine it as **"minimize harm done to any other living beings"**. And guess what. This sentence isn't also the complete one.

# The problems of objectivity and emergence

We are missing one last word in our "formula". And I created a completely separate section for it, because I want to mention two really cool topics.

## Objectivity

We’ve already talked about the subjectivity, but let’s try to figure out, what would be needed to make a fully objective statement on some topic. To achieve that, you would have to be in possession of the full knowledge about the universe. This in turn would require you to know the state of every single  particle in existence. The most optimal way to store all this information would be to reproduce the whole system (our universe). But you cannot do that, because the whole cosmos cannot be squeezed so much, that it could fit inside your skull (well, it could be, but then it's properties would be a little bit different...).

## Emergence

Lately I started a fantastic book "Simply complexity: a clear guide to complexity theory" by Neil Johnson. I have already 63 pages behind me, so I can play a smart-ass. (To be honest it's a great book, really worth reading.) As the title indicates, this book puts emphasis on the description of behavior of complex systems. (You can Google what a "complex system" is for some more information, but it's not necessary to fully understand the rest of this post). One fascinating phenomenon appearing while analyzing these systems must be mentioned here - **emergence**. From Wikipedia we can find out that "emergence occurs when an entity is observed to have properties its parts do not have on their own. These properties or behaviors emerge only when the parts interact in a wider whole". My favorite example of emergence is the **hive mind**. One bee on its own is not capable of doing a lot of complicated things (with all due respect to our noble, yellow friends), but a swarm already is. It can build a huge hive or protect itself against attacks of predators in very sophisticated ways. And all of that thanks to interaction between many, hard-working bees, each of which acts in a definitely simpler way.

And we are in some way similar to the bees. We are not capable of doing as much on our own, as in a group. But it's also hard to foresee the consequences of our actions, while moving to the higher level of abstraction (like to the perspective of the whole society). Just to show how hard it might be, let's look at the Mendeleev's periodic table:

![](/img/posts/2020-02-23-good-person/mendeleev_table.png)

Now comes the tricky part - how do you make a transition from this table, to a living organism? I mean, do you know the necessary arrangement of elements coming from this table to build at least a fully functional bacteria? I definitely don't...

My point is, that there are some sophisticated ways to analyze complex systems. And you should try your best to reflect on your life, learn from your mistakes and try to foresee the consequences of your actions every single day. But sometimes you are just not capable of doing that (because of insufficient knowledge, amount of money or time). But again, this isn't an excuse for you from thinking!

# Last touches

I think, that we can finally write down the complete form of our recipe. It would be **"TRY to minimize harm done to any other living beings"**. Again, just one little word, but as you've seen, there is quite a lot of reasoning behind it. And I will say it again, that this "try" should not be treated as an excuse from putting effort into anything. It should mean, that you should TRY as hard as possible! 

Phew… That was a long one. I don’t believe, that this post will change much in your life, but at least I hope you enjoyed reading it. At the end, I just wanted to mention some of my personal “truths”, that didn’t quite fit previous sections, but are loosely related to already mentioned ideas and just came to my mind while writing:

- always try to think about the bigger picture,
- learn something new all the time. If not for your sake, then for the sake of others (you don’t know, when some knowledge would emerge as useful),
- there is no such thing as “objectively good person”.

OK, I felt so smart, now it's time to quit this bullshit. Thanks for reading!
